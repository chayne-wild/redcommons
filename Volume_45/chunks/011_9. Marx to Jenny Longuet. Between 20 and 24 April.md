# 9. Marx to Jenny Longuet. Between 20 and 24 April 



**[p. 9]**

MARX TO JENNY LONGUET16

IN LONDON

[Ramsgate, between 20 and 24 April 1874] Dear Jennychen,

I am sending the épreuves* today. Please let me have them back after Longuet has looked through them. I shall then make a definitive version of the copy to be sent to Paris.17

Today was the first day I have been able to do even the slightest amount of work. Up to now, despite baths, walks, marvellous air, care with my diet, etc., my condition was even worse than in London. Proof that the matter had reached a crisis point and that it was high time for me to disappear. For the same reason I am delaying my return, for it is absolutely vital for me to be fit for work when I come back. Engels' letter,15 saying that he is arriving today, reminds me of one of the reasons, I SUPPOSE, for your not coming. How is your health? I am convinced that a few weeks at the SEASIDE would restore you completely. At present it is in fact more pleasant and soothing than during the actual season.

I hope that my darling Putty ' will still recognise me. Tell Tussychen that the 'SACRED MUSIC'—sacrée musique, as she translated it—was not actually known by this name among the frivolous Parisians, but that what it referred to had come from Italy, where they have always turned the Divine into a comedy, and was known as 'concerts spirituels'1 in Diderot's day.

Pour la bonne bouchec Grimm retells the following witticism of the Chevalier de Bouffiers:

Adieu, my darling.

Your
